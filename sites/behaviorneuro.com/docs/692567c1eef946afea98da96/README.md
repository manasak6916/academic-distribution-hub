# Neural Circuits of Remembering: Linking Episodic Memory Systems to Everyday Cognitive Function

## Summary
This article provides a comprehensive synthesis of current cognitive neuroscience research concerning the neural substrates of episodic memory and their impact on everyday cognition. It details the specialized yet highly interactive roles of the medial temporal lobe, specifically the hippocampus and perirhinal cortex, in associative binding and item familiarity. The review expands to large-scale neocortical networks, including the default mode and frontoparietal networks, highlighting how their dynamic interactions support memory encoding, consolidation, and retrieval. It also explores the influence of biological variables such as age and sex on neural circuitry, alongside the pathological disruptions observed in conditions like Alzheimer's disease and schizophrenia. Furthermore, the text examines innovative methodologies, including virtual reality assessments, computational generative models, and hierarchical fMRI frameworks, which bridge the gap between laboratory settings and real-world cognitive functioning. Ultimately, this resource offers critical insights for researchers and drug development professionals aiming to identify novel biomarkers and targeted therapeutic interventions for memory-related neurological and psychiatric disorders.

## Extended Summary
This comprehensive whitepaper explores the intricate neural architecture underlying episodic memory and its critical role in everyday cognitive function. At the core of this system is the medial temporal lobe, where the hippocampus acts as a domain-general associative engine for rapid memory encoding and pattern completion, while the perirhinal cortex serves as a domain-specific hub for item feature processing and familiarity. The article emphasizes that episodic memory relies on a continuous, dynamic dialogue between these structures and large-scale neocortical networks, including the default mode, dorsal attention, and ventral attention networks. This hippocampo-neocortical interaction is fundamental to memory consolidation, a process increasingly understood through computational generative models where the hippocampus trains neocortical networks to reconstruct past events and simulate future scenarios.

The review also highlights how fundamental biological variables, specifically age and sex, profoundly modulate these neural circuits, influencing both typical developmental trajectories and the manifestation of cognitive decline. In pathological states such as Alzheimer's disease, schizophrenia, and other neuropsychiatric conditions, disruptions in functional connectivity and structural integrity severely impair episodic memory. For instance, Alzheimer's disease is characterized by early default mode network disconnection and specific degradation of personal semantic memory, shifting the perspective of the disease toward a disconnection syndrome.

To investigate these complex mechanisms, the article details advanced experimental methodologies. Innovative approaches like highly immersive virtual reality paradigms provide ecologically valid assessments of memory in action, bridging the gap between controlled laboratory tasks and real-world cognition. Additionally, hierarchical functional magnetic resonance imaging frameworks and mobile brain and body imaging offer unprecedented insights into the multiscale organization of memory and metacognition. By synthesizing empirical evidence, computational modeling, and clinical applications, this resource provides a foundational guide for researchers and pharmacological developers seeking to identify precise neuromarkers, understand memory distortions, and design targeted therapeutic interventions to mitigate cognitive decline and enhance everyday functioning.
