# PEDOT:PSS for Next-Generation Bioelectronics: From Material Fundamentals to Clinical Neural Interfaces

## Summary
This comprehensive review explores poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):polystyrene sulfonate (PEDOT:PSS) as a transformative conducting polymer for next-generation bioelectronics. Addressing the critical mechanical mismatch between traditional rigid electronics and soft biological tissues, PEDOT:PSS offers an exceptional combination of mixed ionic-electronic conductivity, mechanical flexibility, and biocompatibility. The article details fundamental material chemistry, synthesis methodologies, and advanced fabrication techniques, including 3D printing, spin coating, and electrospinning. It extensively covers performance engineering strategies such as secondary doping with polar solvents, the integration of carbon nanomaterials, and the development of tunable conductive hydrogels to optimize electrical and mechanical properties. Furthermore, the review highlights diverse applications ranging from high-fidelity neural interfaces and deep brain stimulation electrodes to organic electrochemical transistors (OECTs) and wearable biosensors. By addressing biological challenges like biofouling through advanced surface modifications and comparing PEDOT:PSS favorably against traditional noble metals and other conducting polymers, this guide provides researchers with a robust framework for developing advanced, long-lasting bioelectronic therapeutics and diagnostic tools.

## Extended Summary
This extensive technical review provides a deep dive into the utilization of poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):polystyrene sulfonate (PEDOT:PSS) in advanced bioelectronic applications. Traditional bioelectronic materials, such as silicon and noble metals, suffer from a severe mechanical mismatch with soft biological tissues, often triggering foreign body responses and chronic inflammation. PEDOT:PSS overcomes these limitations by offering tissue-like mechanical compliance, excellent biocompatibility, and unique mixed ionic-electronic conductivity, making it an ideal candidate for neural interfaces, wearable sensors, and implantable therapeutics. The article systematically breaks down the molecular blueprint of PEDOT:PSS, explaining how the conductive PEDOT backbone and the insulating PSS polyanion interact to form a water-dispersible, electroactive complex. It outlines diverse synthesis and fabrication methodologies, including chemical and electrochemical polymerization, spin coating, spray coating, and cutting-edge 3D printing techniques tailored for creating complex, tissue-mimicking architectures. A significant portion of the review is dedicated to performance engineering. Pristine PEDOT:PSS often exhibits limited conductivity and environmental instability due to excess insulating PSS. To mitigate this, researchers employ secondary doping with polar solvents (like DMSO and ethylene glycol) or ionic liquids, which reorganizes the polymer morphology to drastically enhance charge transport. Additionally, the integration of carbon nanomaterials (such as graphene and carbon nanotubes) and the formulation of mechanically tunable conductive hydrogels allow for the precise optimization of both electrical performance and mechanical stretchability. The review also addresses practical biological challenges, detailing advanced surface modification strategies, such as zwitterionic peptide coatings, to prevent biofouling and mitigate inflammatory host responses. Finally, comparative analyses demonstrate that PEDOT:PSS outperforms traditional noble metals in micro-scale electrochemical impedance and charge injection capacity, while also offering distinct advantages over other conducting polymers like polypyrrole (PPy) and polyaniline (PANI). Ultimately, this comprehensive guide equips researchers with the foundational knowledge and experimental protocols necessary to translate PEDOT:PSS innovations into clinically viable bioelectronic devices.
