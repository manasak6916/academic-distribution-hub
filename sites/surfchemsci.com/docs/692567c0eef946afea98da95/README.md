# Surface Chemistry Engineering of Perovskite Quantum Dots: Strategies, Applications, and Future Directions in Biomedicine

## Summary
This comprehensive review explores the surface chemistry engineering of perovskite quantum dots (PQDs), a critical frontier in nanomaterials tailored for optoelectronic and biomedical applications. PQDs exhibit exceptional optical properties, such as high photoluminescence quantum yield and tunable bandgaps, but face significant challenges regarding colloidal instability and environmental degradation due to their dynamic ionic lattice and surface defects. To overcome these limitations, researchers have developed advanced surface passivation and ligand exchange strategies. These include the use of multidentate lattice-matched anchoring molecules, 2D perovskite-like ligands, and robust encapsulation matrices like metal-organic frameworks and polymers. The article details experimental synthesis protocols, such as ligand-assisted reprecipitation and hot-injection methods, while highlighting the transition from traditional insulating ligands to functionalized alternatives. Furthermore, it examines the critical role of surface engineering in mitigating heavy metal toxicity and enhancing biocompatibility for clinical translation, specifically in targeted drug delivery and microfluidic biosensing. Ultimately, rational surface manipulation serves as a fundamental design strategy to unlock the commercial and clinical potential of PQDs.

## Extended Summary
Perovskite quantum dots (PQDs) have emerged as transformative semiconductor nanomaterials with exceptional optoelectronic properties, including near-unity photoluminescence quantum yield (PLQY) and tunable emission spectra. However, their commercial and clinical viability is severely hindered by intrinsic instability, primarily driven by dynamic surface ligand binding, halide vacancies, and susceptibility to environmental stressors like moisture and heat. This article provides an in-depth review of surface chemistry engineering strategies designed to reconstruct the PQD surface, thereby enhancing performance, operational stability, and biocompatibility.

The review systematically details the limitations of conventional insulating ligands, such as oleic acid and oleylamine, which readily desorb and create non-radiative recombination centers. To address this, advanced ligand engineering approaches are explored, including multidentate lattice-matched anchoring molecules (e.g., TMeOPPO-p), zwitterionic ligands, and 2D perovskite-like shells. These engineered interfaces provide robust defect passivation, suppress ion migration, and facilitate efficient inter-dot charge transport, leading to record-breaking efficiencies in solar cells and light-emitting diodes. Additionally, the article outlines various encapsulation methodologies, such as silica coating, metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), and polymer matrices, that physically isolate PQDs from degrading elements to achieve exceptional long-term stability.

Beyond optoelectronics, the document emphasizes the critical role of surface functionalization in adapting PQDs for biomedical applications. It provides detailed experimental protocols for synthesizing and modifying PQDs to mitigate heavy metal toxicity, improve aqueous solubility, and enable the covalent conjugation of targeting moieties and therapeutic agents. Case studies highlight the successful deployment of surface-engineered PQDs in targeted drug delivery systems and microfluidic fluorescence-based biosensors. By comparing PQDs with other nanomaterials like graphene quantum dots, the review underscores that rational surface manipulation is not merely a post-synthetic treatment but a foundational design strategy. Ultimately, mastering the PQD-ligand interface is essential for translating these advanced nanomaterials from laboratory curiosities into reliable, commercially viable technologies for next-generation optoelectronics and nanomedicine.
