# A-to-I RNA Editing: From Molecular Mechanism to Therapeutic Application in Biomedicine

## Summary
Adenosine-to-inosine (A-to-I) RNA editing, catalyzed by ADAR enzymes, is the most common post-transcriptional modification in humans. It diversifies the transcriptome and proteome by converting adenosine to inosine in double-stranded RNA (dsRNA), which is then read as guanosine. This mechanism is crucial for normal biological functions, including neural development and innate immunity, where ADAR1 prevents the misidentification of endogenous dsRNA as viral, thus avoiding autoimmune responses. Dysregulation of A-to-I editing is linked to various diseases, including cancer, autoimmune disorders, and neurological conditions. Consequently, there is growing interest in therapeutic applications that leverage programmable guide RNAs to recruit endogenous ADAR enzymes for correcting disease-causing mutations at the RNA level. This approach offers a reversible and potentially safer alternative to permanent genome editing, with significant potential for precision medicine.

## Extended Summary
Adenosine-to-inosine (A-to-I) RNA editing is a fundamental post-transcriptional modification catalyzed by the Adenosine Deaminase Acting on RNA (ADAR) enzyme family. This process involves the hydrolytic deamination of adenosine to inosine within double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) structures, which is subsequently interpreted as guanosine by cellular machinery. This effectively recodes genetic information at the transcript level, expanding proteomic diversity. The ADAR family in mammals includes the catalytically active ADAR1 and ADAR2, and the inactive ADAR3. ADAR1, which is ubiquitously expressed, plays a critical role in innate immunity by editing endogenous dsRNAs (often formed by Alu elements) to mark them as 'self' and prevent activation of antiviral pathways by sensors like MDA5. ADAR2 is predominantly expressed in the nervous system and is vital for editing key neuronal transcripts, such as glutamate receptors, to fine-tune synaptic function. Dysregulation of A-to-I editing is implicated in numerous human diseases. In cancer, altered editing of oncogenes like AZIN1 and tumor suppressors can drive tumor progression and therapy resistance. In autoimmune disorders like Aicardi-Goutieres syndrome, ADAR1 mutations lead to chronic interferon responses. This deep biological understanding has spurred the development of therapeutic platforms that use guide RNAs or chemically modified oligonucleotides to recruit endogenous ADARs to correct pathogenic point mutations, offering a transient and tunable alternative to permanent DNA editing.
