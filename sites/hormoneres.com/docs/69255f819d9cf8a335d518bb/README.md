# IGF-1 and IGF-2 in Fetal Development: From Molecular Mechanisms to Therapeutic Targeting

## Summary
This article provides a comprehensive analysis of Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1) and Insulin-like Growth Factor 2 (IGF-2), detailing their critical roles in fetal development. It explores the foundational biology of the IGF system, including ligands, receptors like IGF1R, and regulatory binding proteins. The text highlights the distinct functions of IGF-1, crucial for CNS maturation, and IGF-2, the primary fetal growth factor, which are regulated by mechanisms like genomic imprinting. Dysregulation of this system leads to severe pathological consequences, including the opposite growth disorders Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (overgrowth) and Silver-Russell Syndrome (growth restriction), as well as major complications of prematurity such as ROP and BPD due to postnatal IGF-1 deficiency. The review also covers experimental methodologies for studying the IGF axis and examines therapeutic strategies, such as receptor-targeted antibodies, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, and regenerative medicine approaches using engineered IGF variants and hydrogel delivery systems.

## Extended Summary
This document offers a detailed review of the Insulin-like Growth Factor (IGF) system, focusing on the distinct and synergistic functions of IGF-1 and IGF-2 in fetal tissue development. It outlines the core components of the system: the ligands (IGF-1, IGF-2), their receptors (IGF1R, IR-A, IGF2R), and the six IGF-binding proteins (IGFBPs) that modulate ligand bioavailability. The text emphasizes the differential roles of the two growth factors; IGF-2 acts as the predominant fetal mitogen, regulated by genomic imprinting at the 11p15.5 locus, while IGF-1 is vital for later fetal growth, central nervous system development, and postnatal maturation. The article details the downstream signaling cascades, primarily the PI3K/Akt and MAPK pathways, which mediate the mitogenic, anti-apoptotic, and metabolic effects of IGFs. Pathological consequences of IGF dysregulation are thoroughly examined, including the clinically opposite imprinting disorders Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (BWS) and Silver-Russell Syndrome (SRS), complications of prematurity linked to postnatal IGF-1 deficiency, and non-islet cell tumor hypoglycemia (NICTH) caused by excess pro-IGF2. Furthermore, the document reviews a range of experimental methodologies for studying the IGF axis and explores current and emerging therapeutic strategies. These include receptor-targeted monoclonal antibodies, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, engineered IGF variants with modified IGFBP affinity, and regenerative medicine applications involving sustained delivery of IGF-1 via fibrin hydrogels.
