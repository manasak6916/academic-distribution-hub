# Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS): Principles, Current Research, and Strategic Imperatives for Long-Duration Space Missions

## Summary
This article provides a comprehensive analysis of Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS), which are closed-loop artificial ecosystems designed to sustain human life on long-duration space missions. By integrating biological components like plants, microorganisms, and sometimes animals, BLSS mimic Earth's ecology to regenerate oxygen, water, and food while recycling waste. The text examines the historical development of BLSS, highlighting a strategic divergence where NASA discontinued its integrated programs like BIO-PLEX, creating a capability gap that China has since filled, achieving world records with its Lunar Palace 1 facility. Key technical challenges are explored, including mass transfer limitations in partial gravity, microbial contamination, the effects of space radiation on biological components, and the need for advanced control systems. The document details various research methodologies, from ground-based analog testing to predictive modeling, and assesses the technology readiness levels of different BLSS subsystems, emphasizing the strategic importance of these systems for future Moon and Mars missions.

## Extended Summary
This comprehensive review details the principles, research, and strategic importance of Bioregenerative Life Support Systems (BLSS) for sustainable human space exploration. BLSS are engineered, closed-loop ecosystems that mimic Earth's natural cycles to regenerate air, water, and food from waste products, thereby enabling long-duration missions to the Moon and Mars with minimal reliance on terrestrial resupply. The document traces the historical evolution of BLSS, from early Soviet BIOS and American CELSS programs to the ambitious Biosphere 2 project. It critically analyzes the strategic divergence in the early 2000s, when NASA discontinued its integrated BIO-PLEX program, creating a capability gap. This shift allowed China to become the global leader, demonstrated by the landmark 'Lunar Palace 365' mission, which sustained a crew for 370 days with over 98% material closure.

The article provides a deep dive into the technical architecture of BLSS, comparing the compartmentalized, microbial-focused approach of ESA's MELiSSA program with the integrated, multi-trophic system of China's Lunar Palace. It examines the roles of key biological components, including higher plants for food and oxygen, microbial bioreactors for waste processing, and novel components like aquatic bryophytes for biofiltration and insects for protein production. Significant technical challenges are addressed, such as the impact of partial gravity on mass transfer, microbial contamination management, the detrimental effects of space radiation on biological systems, and the necessity of advanced control strategies like Model Predictive Control (MPC) to manage system stability. The text also covers the synergy between BLSS and In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU), using local materials like regolith to enhance self-sufficiency.
