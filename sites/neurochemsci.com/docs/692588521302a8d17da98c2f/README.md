# Microglial Activation in Mental Disorders: From Neuroinflammatory Mechanisms to Novel Therapeutic Strategies

## Summary
This comprehensive review explores the critical role of microglial activation and neuroinflammation in the pathophysiology of mental disorders, such as major depressive disorder and schizophrenia. Moving beyond the oversimplified M1/M2 dichotomy, the article highlights the complex, context-dependent states of microglia, including disease-associated phenotypes. It details key signaling pathways governing microglial homeostasis and reactivity, notably TREM2, mTOR, and NF-κB. The review also examines advanced methodological approaches for studying microglial function, ranging from single-cell transcriptomics and high-content screening to in vivo models and TSPO PET imaging. Furthermore, it addresses translational challenges like blood-brain barrier penetration and the management of immune-related adverse events. Finally, the article evaluates promising therapeutic strategies, including TREM2 agonists, SHIP1/INPP5D inhibitors, and natural compounds like costunolide and osthole, emphasizing the potential of precision medicine and combination therapies to restore microglial homeostasis and treat neuroinflammatory conditions.

## Extended Summary
This extensive review synthesizes current research on microglial activation and neuroinflammation as central mechanisms in the pathogenesis of mental and neurodegenerative disorders, including major depressive disorder, schizophrenia, and Alzheimer's disease. Historically classified into binary pro-inflammatory (M1) and anti-inflammatory (M2) states, microglia are now understood to exist across a highly dynamic continuum. Advanced single-cell omics and spatial transcriptomics have revealed context-specific phenotypes, such as disease-associated microglia (DAM), which play dual neuroprotective and neurotoxic roles depending on the disease stage.

The article provides a deep dive into the molecular pathways regulating microglial function, highlighting the critical roles of TREM2, Akt/mTOR, NF-κB, and the NLRP3 inflammasome. It systematically outlines experimental methodologies essential for neuroimmunology research, including in vitro high-content screening using BV2 cells, in vivo models like chronic restraint stress and photothrombotic stroke, and advanced biomarker detection methods such as cerebrospinal fluid sTREM2 quantification and TSPO PET imaging.

Translational challenges from bench to bedside are critically examined, particularly the difficulty of achieving blood-brain barrier penetration. The review discusses innovative delivery systems, including lipid nanoparticles and receptor-mediated transcytosis, to overcome these barriers. Additionally, it addresses the intersection of neuroinflammation and oncology, exploring how immune checkpoint inhibitors induce neurological immune-related adverse events through mechanisms overlapping with psychiatric pathophysiology.

Finally, the review evaluates the current therapeutic pipeline targeting microglial dysregulation. It contrasts the clinical failure of first-generation TREM2 antibodies like AL002 with the promise of next-generation candidates such as VHB937 and VG-3927. It also highlights the efficacy of small molecules and natural products like costunolide and osthole, alongside novel targets including SHIP1/INPP5D, TRPV2, NEU1, and CD33. Ultimately, the article advocates for precision medicine and combination therapies that synergize microglial modulators with disease-modifying agents to effectively restore neural homeostasis and treat complex CNS disorders.
